Calibrating gyrochronology using Kepler asteroseismic targets

Stars are expected to spin down as they age in a predictable way. In theory FGK stars `forget' their initial rotation periods and, provided a star is older than about 500 million years, you can predict its current rotation period, given estimates of its age and mass. Inversely, given a rotation period and mass estimate, you can predict its age. This method of inferring a stellar age, given a rotation period is called gyrochronology. We used asteroseismic ages for around 300 Kepler field stars from Chaplin et al. (2014), with rotation periods from Garcia et al. (2014), to calibrate gyrochronology for old field stars. It seems as though stellar ages predicted by gyrochronology do not agree with those predicted by asteroseismology for these stars (see image right). The stars plotted are around solar mass and the old ones fall below the models that were calibrated using the the Sun (red) and nearby field stars (blue). Gyrochronology predicts that these stars are younger than their asteroseismic ages. Or put another way, these stars are too rapidly rotating for their age and mass. Are these stars binaries? Are the rotation periods wrong? Are the asteroseismic ages wrong? Are the models wrong? We still don't know the answers to these questions!

The SIP

The Kepler spacecraft only has two functioning reaction wheels left and its pointing is not what it used to be. Correcting the large pointing-induced signal that appear in the repurposed Kepler mission (K2) light curves is an important but delicate operation. The Systematics-Insensitive Periodogram (SIP) is a simple extension of a sine-fitting periodogram, where instead of modelling the data with a sinusoidal signal alone, you model the data with a sinusoid plus a noise model. This technique avoids the necessity for directly detrending the light curves and should be better for detecting low amplitude signals in the data than a traditional 'detrend-then-compute-a-periodogram' approach.
